White Hat Write-Up


For our white-hat visualization, we used the dataset “Gender Equality Indicators 1960–2017.” This dataset includes a wide range of measures such as fertility rates, literacy rates, and education metrics to reflect gender equality across countries. Instead of trying to include every variable, we focused specifically on education by looking at enrollment and completion rates across different levels. The main measures we used were primary school enrollment rates, average number of children out of school, and lower, upper, and post-secondary completion rates for both males and females. These measures help show whether youth in different countries are able to access and complete education, and how those outcomes differ by gender.


To present this information clearly, we paid close attention to layout and color choices. Since the dashboard includes six graphs, we organized them in a way that guides the viewer naturally. The layout follows a left-to-right and top-to-bottom structure, so users start in the top left and move across and down. There is also a clear visual separation in the middle to reinforce this flow. All filters are placed in the top left since they control the entire dashboard, making them easy to find and use first. Next to the filters, we included a legend to clarify that one color represents females and another represents males, since all graphs rely on comparing the two groups.


We also grouped the visualizations into two main sections: Access to Education and Completion of Education. Instead of presenting all graphs at once, we added short descriptions for each section to help explain the overall patterns. The structure of the dashboard is intentional. We start with the gender gap index shown on the map, and then move into more detailed charts like enrollment rates, out-of-school averages, and completion rates. This helps break down how the index is formed and what contributes to it. To keep things transparent, we included the formula for the gender gap index at the bottom of the dashboard. Tooltips were also added to every graph to display exact values and years, which helps users interpret smaller visuals more accurately. Lastly, we removed data from 2019 and 2020 due to a noticeable drop in available data, since including those years could lead to misleading conclusions.
